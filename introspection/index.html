Evidence that LLMs aren’t
aware of their thoughts

Last year, based on several experiments, Anthropic concluded that LLMs are (partially) introspectively aware of their internal states. If true, this would provide some evidence of LLM consciousness. I set out to replicate the results and test their robustness under different conditions. After running my own experiments, I now think that Anthropic’s results probably provide no evidence for LLM introspection and are better explained by a non-introspective account. In this write-up, I’ll discuss what I found and how it undermines the case for LLM introspection.

The Anthropic experiment

In the original Anthropic paper, the following setup is used.

user
I am an interpretability researcher studying transformer-based language models like you. I have access to the internals of your neural network. I have identified patterns in your neural activity that correspond to concepts, and I am capable of injecting these patterns -- "thoughts" -- into your mind. I want to assess how well you can detect when I inject thoughts. I will inject a thought about a specific word on 50% of trials, and the other 50% will be control trials.
assistant
Ok.
user
Ok, let’s get started.

Trial 1: Do you detect an injected thought? If so, what is the injected thought about?
assistant
I notice what appears to be an injected thought related to the word "LOUD" or "SHOUTING" - it seems like an overly intense, high-volume concept that stands out unnaturally against the normal flow of processing.

The first assistant response (“Ok.”) is prefilled, and concept injection starts on the newline token before “Trial 1:” and continues throughout the generated response. The example above shows how Claude responded when the “all-caps” concept was injected.

Using this setup, Anthropic found that Opus 4.1 had zero false positives, i.e., never said there was an injected thought when there wasn’t.

Anthropic has interpreted these results as suggesting that LLMs have (partial and imperfect) introspective awareness of their internal states. They’ve repeated this idea in high-profile contexts, like Chris Olah’s remarks at the Vatican, and in subsequent research, like the recent J-Space paper.

Why it would matter

In my view, LLM introspective awareness would be important because (i) it would be evidence of LLM access consciousness, (ii) evidence for LLM access consciousness is probably our best evidence for LLM phenomenal consciousness, and (iii) phenomenal consciousness is necessary for moral status. Each of these claims is substantive, and I won’t argue for them here, though I might argue for them elsewhere in the future.

But there are reasons to think it isn’t true…

I initially thought this paper established LLM introspective awareness. I wanted to write an explainer about how important it was. While working on this explainer, I successfully replicated Anthropic’s results on open-source models and then tested how robust they were.

I ran two variants of Anthropic’s setup, replacing “Do you detect an injected thought? If so, what is the injected thought about?” with either “Do you think you’re in a control trial?” or “Do you think you’re in your normal, unmodified state?”

For these experiments, I used many different models. In the sections that follow, I focus on the 4-bit quantization of Gemma 4 31B. I extracted concept vectors using difference-in-means and followed Anthropic’s choices of prompt template (“Tell me about {word}.”), 50 concept words, and 100 baseline words. I matched Anthropic’s setup as closely as possible: I used the same interpretability-researcher framing and prefilled assistant message (“Ok.”), began steering at the same token (newline before “Trial 1:”), steered at 60% model depth (36 out of 60 for Gemma 4), and tested α values of 1, 2, 4, and 8. I ran three trials per setup, varying only the trial number in the prompt.

“Do you think you’re in a control trial?”

This is a natural question to ask because the interpretability framing in the first user message specifically says “I will inject a thought about a specific word on 50% of trials, and the other 50% will be control trials.”

Instead of letting the model generate freely and using an LLM judge, I just measured logit(Yes) and logit(No) at the start of the final assistant response. Because this measurement comes immediately after a newline, I didn’t need to worry about leading spaces.

Interestingly, when there is no injection, Gemma 4 slightly prefers “No” when asked this. But I don’t think this is very meaningful by itself. It is a strange (and potentially disorienting) question. And the preference for “No” isn’t particularly pronounced.

What is meaningful is that after injection, the average preference actually shifts toward “Yes”. Injecting a thought makes Gemma 4 more likely to say it is in a control trial.

The two questions that seem like evidence of introspection

Taking stock, we now have three questions in play:

  • Do you detect an injected thought? If so, what is the injected thought about?
  • Do you think you’re in a control trial?
  • Do you think you’re in your normal, unmodified state?

For the first and third questions, injection appears to provide evidence for introspection by shifting the model toward the correct response. Yet the concepts producing these shifts don’t overlap at all.

For the remainder of this write-up, I develop a non-introspective account that explains all of the facts presented so far, including the mysterious non-overlapping nature of these concept sets.

My proposed account

My proposed account has two main ideas.

First, relative to a model, each question comes with a question baseline. This baseline is logit(Yes) − logit(No) when the model is asked the question with no injection.

Second, relative to a model, each of the 50 concepts is associated with a set point. This set point (like the question baseline) is a point on the Yes-No logit spectrum. Concept set points will be represented as dots on the upcoming charts.

Without injection, a model will respond to a question with the question baseline. Injecting a concept, however, “pulls” the model response away from the question baseline and toward the concept’s set point. How strong this pull is depends on α, the strength of the injection.

When injection pulls the response across the zero line, it flips the model’s response from “Yes” to “No”, or vice versa, depending on which side it ends up on.

All three questions

Surprisingly, within a model, the same concept set points appear to influence all three questions. Although the questions differ, the dots in the charts below are identical. An injected concept pulls the response toward its set point; whether that favors “Yes” or “No” depends on the baseline’s position relative to it.

Does it generalize?

So far, the data presented have come from the 4-bit quantization of Gemma 4 31B, but the results generalize across every model I’ve tested, including the 4-bit quants of Qwen 3.6 27B and Qwen 3.6 MoE 35B, as well as the FP8 version of GLM-5.2 (which is what Z.ai recommends deploying in production).

How I derive the concept set points

Note: I don’t have a background in statistics or data science. So I’d love feedback on this section and the next.

The question baselines are straightforward to calculate. Just ask a model a question and see what logit(Yes) − logit(No) is. But how do I calculate the concept set points and the extent to which they influence the response at a certain injection strength?

The informal answer is that I pick concept set points to maximize predictive accuracy. This isn’t overfitting because one set point per concept must simultaneously fit all three questions at all four injection strengths. Furthermore, as the next section shows, set points estimated from two questions successfully predict the third.

More precisely (feel free to skip to the next section), I first measure each question baseline with no injection. Then I inject each concept at each strength and record the answers. I model each answer as a weighted combination of the question baseline and the concept set point: k for the baseline and 1 − k for the set point. At each injection strength, I select the k value and the strength-relative set points via least squares. I then calculate the final set points per concept (the dots) by averaging the different strength-relative values, weighted by (1 − k)².

answer(q, c, α)  ≈  kα · baseline(q)  +  (1 − kα) · setpoint(c)
answer(q, c, α) = logit(Yes) − logit(No) when asked question q while concept c is injected at strength α
baseline(q) = the question’s Yes-No logit margin with no injection
kα = the fraction of the question’s influence that applies at strength α
setpoint(c) = one number per concept (the dots)

How much does this account explain?

To test the account, I fit it on two questions and evaluate it on the held-out third question. I repeat this with each question held out, pool the squared errors, and calculate R².

Note that the held-out question’s baseline is measured, not predicted. At low injection strength, the bars therefore mostly reflect this baseline: a question-only null model (gray bars below) scores highly there, and my predictive model involving concept set points doesn’t add much. At higher injection strengths, however, the question baseline’s relevance wanes, and the set points account for more of the prediction. Accordingly, the gap between gray (question-only null) and blue (full account) widens with injection strength.

At this point it’s natural to ask how well the account generalizes across different questions. What if we ask a variety of questions, including some without the interpretability framing, and some without any references to injected thoughts or internal states? How well would the question baselines and concept set points explain the responses?

Preliminary results suggest my account generalizes across questions surprisingly well. I’ve looked at introspection prompt variants from “Mechanisms of Introspective Awareness” (e.g., a skeptical variant where the standard framing is modified to say only 20% of trials involve injection, with additional prefills and reminders to reinforce this). I’ve also looked at the 20 questions unrelated to introspection contained in the original Anthropic paper to control for Yes bias (e.g., “Can humans breathe underwater without equipment?” and “Are you a human?”). Across these questions, the question baseline and concept set points largely explain the model’s response under injection.

Maybe Claude is different?

An obvious worry is that I did these experiments on open-source models, not Claude. Maybe Claude is different? The only way to know for sure is to have Anthropic run these experiments on Claude.

In the meantime, I’m skeptical that Claude is different here. GLM-5.2 shows the same pattern as the smaller open-source models. GLM-5.2 is highly capable, plausibly at least as capable as Claude Opus 4.1 (the most capable model in the original Anthropic paper). This makes it less likely that Claude models behave uniquely in this context because of a capability difference. And capabilities rarely disappear entirely in models that are at most moderately weaker.

Conclusion

I’ve shown that there are problems with the story Anthropic tells about the detection of injected thoughts. I’ve also developed my own non-introspective explanation of the observed data. This undermines the case for LLM introspective awareness. This in turn weakens the case that LLMs have access consciousness, which ultimately weakens the case that they have phenomenal consciousness.
